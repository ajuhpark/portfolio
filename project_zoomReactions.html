'Zoom' Reactions
DESCRIPTION

Zoom is an amazing tool to connect and, at the same time, users can still feel limited in what they're expressing over video. I wanted to design a feature that would allow users to communicate gifs and emojis in a viewable format for everyone in the meeting.

OVERVIEW

Problem

Video chats have become a norm among friends and coworkers but there's still room in the chats for people to express more and communicate on different levels. The work of processing non-verbal cues along with the frequency of meetings can cause users to experience Zoom fatigue.

Solution

To improve the interactions and alleviate Zoom fatigue, we can add layers of communication to make users feel more connected and engaged. The area Iâ€™m focusing on is the ability to react using gifs and emojis within the videochat. Adding this of communication will give people more cues to latch onto and allow people to express more of themselves in the meeting.

USER RESEARCH

User Survey

I created a survey to better understand how people use gifs and emojis and how they use Zoom. These are a few of the results and takeaways I had from the survey:

Of the users surveyed, 86.7% believed that emojis and gifs expressed emotions that written messages, voice and video could not express. This showed me that people viewed emojis and gifs as expressive tools that other modes of communication couldn't exactly replicate.

I also asked about the context in which users used emojis and gifs and there were a wide range of reasons. All of the participants used it 'to lighten the mood' and the second and third most popular answers were 'to express frustration' and 'to show support'. All of these emotions would be useful in increasing user engagement and decreasing fatigue in video chats.

It was also interesting to find that 53.3% of participants either did not use the reaction feature in Zoom or didn't know there was a reaction feature. This showed me that there was room to make the feature more appealing and usable.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

I took a look at two apps that were similar to Zoom in its utilization of videos and emojis - Youtube Live and Twitch. Both have a window for video with a side chat bar with an emoji menu.

Youtube Live

In Youtube Live, the chat shows up next to the video as a default. Most people are able to see the messages and emojis because the chat is a large part of the interactive experience.

On the other hand, the chat function in Zoom is hidden as a default and used more sparingly. Most of the interaction in Zoom is focused in the video window. Since viewers would mainly be looking at the video window of the app, I wanted to look further into how people could see emojis and reactions without having to open the chat side bar.

Twitch

Twitch is similar to Youtube Live in that there's only one video screen and people are reacting to it in the chat. But one feature that differentiates it is an available widget that allows users to show emojis in the video window by entering them in the chat. In the image below, there are emojis surround the DJ.

This emoji feature brings a new aspect of user interaction. People can more easily show support and it makes for a more fun environment when everyone can partake in a shared experience. The added challenge with Zoom was being able to view user reactions when there are multiple video screens.

Reaction Menu

The current Zoom reaction menu appears when the user clicks on the 'Reactions' button on the bottom bar. It fits in well with the other icons and is easily accessible so I wanted to build on it. The menu currently only holds six emojis so I looked to expand it. I looked at the emoji and gif menus in different apps.

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

There were two main parts I wanted to tackle when integrating emojis and gifs into Zoom - the menu to choose the reactions and the placement of the reactions in the video chat.

Reaction Menu

Here is a display of the new Zoom reaction menu I created along with still images on the bottom. The current Zoom emoji menu holds six emojis so I wanted to expand on it. I decided on 30 emojis in the menu because I wanted users to have choices but not to the point where they would have a hard time deciding which emoji to use. I included a section near the top of the emoji section where a userâ€™s five most frequently used emojis would appear.

I also included tabs to separate the reactions into two different categories - emojis and gifs. In the gif tab, I included a search bar. The default gifs that showed would be the frequently used gifs. Once users search, they would be able to scroll in a two column collection of gifs.

Reaction Placement

Next, I wanted to figure out where to show these reactions so it would be easily viewable by everyone. In the current Zoom client, once the user clicks on an emoji it's shown on the user's video screen on the top left (as shown in image). In larger Zoom meetings, people's attention would be going back and forth from the speaker to the users who are posting reactions on their own screens.

I wanted people's reactions to be viewed in an expected place so users wouldn't have to move their attention in so many different directions. These are a couple solutions I came up with:

Thumbs Up Reaction in Current Zoom

v1

One solution I came up with was using a conveyor belt to roll out the reaction. I got this inspiration from sushi restaurants that have revolving belts that hold different types of sushi. If something's put on the conveyor belt, it can have its time in the sun and be viewable for a set amount of time. Users would know where to expect the reactions.

Conveyor Belt Solution

v2

The other solution I came up with, and ultimately decided to move forward with, was having a separate screen designated just for emojis and gifs, shown here in a yellow circle. When a user posts a reaction, a bot would enter the videochat with its own screen and show the reaction. The bot's screen would stay until the end of the chat.

Zoom Bot Solution

Zoom Bot Screen Content

I split the Zoom bot window into two sections, one for gifs and one for emojis. I wanted to make sure there was enough room in the window to view the gifs and emojis at the same time as well as the name of the user that posted it.

Gifs would be displayed one at a time for 7 seconds and they would cue if multiple people posted gifs around the same time. When people are not throwing reactions in the bot screen, the default will be text of the date and time.

TESTING

Usability Testing

When I was user testing, one of the users asked what this feature would look like when Zoom was in Spotlight mode. To give background, when Zoom is in Spotlight, it makes one video screen large and puts the other usersâ€™ screens in a bar.

I thought the best way to make the reaction visible was to make the bot screen act like a speaker. Normally when someone speaks, their placement is moved up in the bar. So when someone put in a reaction, the bot screen would activate and move up in priority on the bar.

But the information was small and difficult to see when the bot screen was in the bar, in particular the emojis. To solve this, I decided to have the emoji or gif to take up the whole screen instead of just part of the screen.

Conclusion

This project was fun to tackle because Zoom is a product that I'm using with people nearly every day. The goal for this project was to bring levity and more expressive ways to communicate so users could be more themselves and have a more interactive Zoom experience.

While using Zoom, I found that it was difficult for gifs and emojis to be viewable for all users and if users decided to enter reactions in the chat sidebar, it would stay there and run the risk of not fitting the mood only a few seconds later. Users don't want an Office gif of Michael Scott raising the roof to stay in the chat when the conversation transitions to talk of a financial downturn in the company. I tried to make a version that would have gifs and emojis that only show temporarily so that users could react to the moment.

The most difficult part of the project was coming up with different solutions. I had to think about what was possible within Zoomâ€™s guidelines and then see where I could stretch the app. I checked in with my developer friends to make sure the solution I came up with was plausible and that it wouldnâ€™t take an overhaul of the app to implement. Feedback from users encouraged me along the way, some saying that they only talk in gifs. Hopefully a feature similar to this can be added in the future to make for a more personal video chat experience.
