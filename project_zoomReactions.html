'Zoom' Reactions
DESCRIPTION

Zoom is an amazing tool and at the same time, users can still feel limited in what they're expressing over video. I wanted to design a feature that would allow users to communicate gifs and emojis in a viewable format for everyone in the meeting.

OVERVIEW

Background

Video chats have become a norm among friends and coworkers and there's still room for people to express and communicate on different levels in the chats. I wanted to design a feature for the Zoom client where users would be able to communicate gifs and emojis in a viewable format for everyone in the meeting.

Problem

While the ability to communicate via video is an incredible tool, users can still feel limited in what they're expressing and in their methods of communication. The work of processing non-verbal cues along with the frequency of meetings can cause users to experience Zoom fatigue.

Solution

To improve the interactions and alleviate Zoom fatigue, we can add layers of communication to make users feel more connected and engaged. The area I’m focusing on is the ability to react using gifs and emojis within the videochat. Adding this of communication will give people more cues to latch onto and allow people to express more of themselves in the meeting.

RESEARCH

User Survey

I started with research and began by creating a survey and the participants included a range of genders and gender preferences for dating. These were a couple takeaways I had.

I asked users when they feel like it was no longer appropriate to ghost someone. More than half the people marked that it was never appropriate to ghost. On the other end of the spectrum, three people marked that ghosting would be appropriate up until 2 dates - these were two men and a nonbinary person. Women had a stronger dislike for ghosting than men in the survey.

I also wanted to get a sense of how people felt about texting so I asked participants how much more thought was put into text conversations with their matches relative to their normal conversations. More than half the people responded that texts with their matches required more thought. With that in mind, I thought there was room to lessen thought and anxiety in that space and create more opportunity for natural and flowing conversations.

Competitive Analysis

I took a look at two apps that were similar to Zoom in its utilization of videos and emojis - Youtube Live and Twitch. Both have a window for video with a side chat bar.

Youtube Live

In Youtube Live, the chat is a prominent part of the experience. It shows up next to the video as a default. Most people are able to see the messages and emojis because the chat is a large part of the interactive experience.

On the other hand, the chat function in Zoom is hidden as a default and used more sparingly. Most of the interaction is focused in the video window. Since viewers would mainly be looking at the video window of the app, I wanted to look further into how people can see emojis and reactions without having to open the chat side bar.

Hinge

Hinge’s strength was its clean and modern user interface. They also offered different mediums of communication. In this screenshot, they emphasize their in-app video capabilities.

Goat Date

Goatdate is an app that focuses on video chatting after matching. After users match, they could call their match for a five minute video chat. I really liked the idea for time limited video chats and wanted to build on it by giving users a chance to schedule their meetings.

In Goatdate, users could call at any time but there’s a chance that the call comes in at a time that doesn’t work for the other user. I thought there was an opportunity there to be more intentional by setting aside a set time much like how people approach business meetings.

BRANDING

Logo

For the logo, I wanted to give the idea that they would meet in “The Ring”, kind of like a boxing match. I looked at MMA, boxing and video game designs for inspiration.

I came up with the logo below. I chose Fira Code as the logo font because it gave a strong look that wasn’t too romantic or flowery. There’s a small clash where the two ends meet. And from a distance the sparks can also look like a diamond ring.

Fonts

Fira Code

Fira Code is a monospaced typeface optimized for reading and writing code. It takes less amount of energy to scan and parse the text (and hopefully the user’s matches in the app).

Fira Sans

Fira Sans aims to cover the legibility needs for a large range of handsets varying in screen quality and rendering.

Color

"The Ring" uses a mostly blue palette. The shades of blue give a sense of order and calmness. It’s designed to give a feeling of trust and reduce anxiety in online-dating.

TESTING

After mocking up designs for the screens, I did some usability testing and I wanted to highlight a few things that I learned in that process. Here are a couple examples of changes I made.

Calendar

v1

The calendar screen was an important part of the scheduling process. In the first version, after a user would match, they would be taken to this calendar screen where they could propose a time to meet. I had instructions on the top to place two five minute time slots on Martha's calendar.

From the feedback I received, it wasn’t intuitive for the users to place the two bars on the calendar. One user wasn't sure what the yellow bars were and thought it wasn't related to the calendar because it was a different color. They also noted that there was no instruction to drag or drop the bars.

v2

So I changed the interaction instructions to the following: "Tap and place 2 time slots on Martha's calendar". I put the ‘2’ in bold to emphasize that they need to place two times.

In the new interaction users could tap a time on the calendar and there would be a confirm button that pops up that they could click. Once they confirm, the bar would be set there and they would be able to place another bar in the same way. This was an easier way for users to place times on the calendar.

Ending the Chat

v1

Another part of the app that took a few iterations was the user flow for when a user would want to end a chat.

The user had two minutes do decide whether or not to extend the chat. If the user decided not to extend the chat, it would bring them to a screen where they could send a last message. The intention in putting this screen in was to reduce instances of ghosting and to encourage users to give closure even to this short interaction. I also put templates that the user could swipe through and copy to make it easier.

Once they confirmed, they would be taken to the screen where they could meet more potential matches.

v2

There were a couple things that came up in my usability test. First was the time limit. When one user saw that they had two minutes to write an ending message, they didn't like the pressure. Second was having templates for the ending message. A participant pointed out that if they went with a template, the other person might appreciate it the first time around but if they keep receiving messages that followed a template, it could lose its sincerity.

In this case, less was more. I took out the option to add an ending message - two minutes was sufficient time to decide whether or not to extend but not enough time to send a message. In place of the separate screen to send an ending message, I had them go directly back into the chat where they could send a message if they wanted as opposed to going directly into finding new potential matches.

Conclusion

'The Ring' was an idea I wanted to explore while chatting with my friends about online dating experiences. There are a lot of great apps out there that design for people to match but leave it open thereafter. I thought the feature of scheduling a five minute meet up via text, call or video could give more structure to users for their experience after they match and, while not for everyone, would give users a quicker way to get to know someone.

Usability testing was especially helpful. It expanded my perspective on what users might be thinking and who this app might be good for. For example, one of the people that helped me test the app mentioned that users might place a higher bar on who they select because they'll eventually have to talk to them, something that I hadn't thought about before. It was also helpful to hear from usability testers that they thought the feature would help address the frustrations of people who want to get to know people more quickly.

Thinking through the different cases was a fun exercise because it involved getting into the mind of the users and thinking about the different ways they could react to the other person's choices. The problem solving for 'The Ring' was a fun process and hopefully it's a feature that will help people have less stressful and more intentional experiences in online dating.
