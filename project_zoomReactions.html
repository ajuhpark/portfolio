'Zoom' Reactions
DESCRIPTION

Zoom is an amazing tool and at the same time, users can still feel limited in what they're expressing over video. I wanted to design a feature that would allow users to communicate gifs and emojis in a viewable format for everyone in the meeting.

OVERVIEW

Background

Video chats have become a norm among friends and coworkers but there's still room in the chats for people to express more and communicate on different levels. I wanted to design a feature for the Zoom client where users would be able to communicate gifs and emojis in a viewable format for everyone in the meeting.

Problem

While the ability to communicate via video is an incredible tool, users can still feel limited in what they're expressing and in their methods of communication. The work of processing non-verbal cues along with the frequency of meetings can cause users to experience Zoom fatigue.

Solution

To improve the interactions and alleviate Zoom fatigue, we can add layers of communication to make users feel more connected and engaged. The area I’m focusing on is the ability to react using gifs and emojis within the videochat. Adding this of communication will give people more cues to latch onto and allow people to express more of themselves in the meeting.

RESEARCH

User Survey

I started with research and began by creating a survey and the participants included a range of genders and gender preferences for dating. These were a couple takeaways I had.

I asked users when they feel like it was no longer appropriate to ghost someone. More than half the people marked that it was never appropriate to ghost. On the other end of the spectrum, three people marked that ghosting would be appropriate up until 2 dates - these were two men and a nonbinary person. Women had a stronger dislike for ghosting than men in the survey.

I also wanted to get a sense of how people felt about texting so I asked participants how much more thought was put into text conversations with their matches relative to their normal conversations. More than half the people responded that texts with their matches required more thought. With that in mind, I thought there was room to lessen thought and anxiety in that space and create more opportunity for natural and flowing conversations.

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

I took a look at two apps that were similar to Zoom in its utilization of videos and emojis - Youtube Live and Twitch. Both have a window for video with a side chat bar.

Youtube Live

In Youtube Live, the chat is a prominent part of the experience. It shows up next to the video as a default. Most people are able to see the messages and emojis because the chat is a large part of the interactive experience.

On the other hand, the chat function in Zoom is hidden as a default and used more sparingly. Most of the interaction in Zoom is focused in the video window. Since viewers would mainly be looking at the video window of the app, I wanted to look further into how people could see emojis and reactions without having to open the chat side bar.

Twitch

Twitch is similar to Youtube Live in that there's only one video screen and people are reacting to it in the chat. But one feature that differentiates it is an available widget that allows users to show emojis in the video window by entering them in the chat. In the image below, there are emojis surround the DJ.

This emoji feature brings a new aspect of user interaction. People can more easily show support and it makes for a more fun environment when everyone can view and partake in a shared experience. The added challenge with Zoom was being able to view user reactions when there are multiple video screens. I began working on where I could place the reactions.

Reaction Menu

The current Zoom reaction menu expands when the user clicks on the 'Reactions' button on the bottom bar. It fits in well with the other icons and is easily accessible so I wanted to build on it. The menu currently only holds six emojis so I looked to expand it. I looked at the emoji and gif menus in different apps.

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

There were two main parts I wanted to tackle when integrating emojis and gifs into Zoom - the menu to choose the reactions and the placement of the reactions in the video chat.

Reaction Menu

The current Zoom emoji menu holds six emojis so I wanted to expand on it. I tried not to expand it to the point that users would have to go through pages of emojis or would have a hard time deciding which emoji to use so I limited the menu to 30 emojis. I include a section near the top where a user’s five most frequently used emojis would appear.

I also included tabs to separate the reactions into two different categories - emojis and gifs. In the gif tab, I included a search bar. The default would be the frequently used gifs. Once users search, they'll be able to scroll in a two column collection of gifs.

Reaction Placement

In the current Zoom client, once the user clicks on an emoji it's shown on the user's video screen on the top left. In larger Zoom meetings, people's attention would be going back and forth from the speaker to the users who are posting reactions on their own screens.

I wanted people's reactions to be viewed in an expected place so users wouldn't have to move their attention in so many different directions. These are a couple solutions I came up with:

Thumbs Up Reaction in Current Zoom

v1

One solution I came up with was using a conveyor belt to roll out the reaction. I got this inspiration from sushi restaurants that have revolving belts that hold different types of sushi. If something's put on the conveyor belt, it can have its time in the sun and be viewable for a set amount of time. It would also be slightly less distracting than a reaction moving on different directions of the zoom screen.

Conveyor Belt Solution

v2

The solution I came up and ultimately decided to move forward with was having a separate screen designated just for emojis and gifs. When someone posts a reaction, a bot would enter the videochat with its own screen and show the reaction. The bot's screen would stay until the end of the chat.

Zoom Bot Solution

Zoom Bot Screen Content

I split the Zoom bot window into two sections, one for gifs and one for emojis. I wanted to make sure there was enough room in the window to view the gifs and emojis at the same time as well as the name of the user that posted it.

Gifs would be displayed one at a time for 7 seconds and they would cue if multiple people posted gifs around the same time. When people are not throwing reactions in the bot screen, the default will be text of the date and time.

TESTING

Usability Testing

When I was user testing, one of the users asked what this feature would look like when Zoom was in Spotlight mode. When Zoom is in Spotlight, it makes one video screen large and puts the users’ screens in a bar.

I thought the best way to make the bot screen act was like a speaker. Normally when someone speaks, their placement is moved up in the bar. So when someone put in a reaction, it would activate and move up in priority on the bar.

But the information was small and difficult to see when the bot screen was in the bar, in particular the emojis. To solve this, I decided to have it take up the whole screen instead of just part of the screen.

Conclusion

This project was fun to tackle because Zoom is a product that I'm using with people nearly every day. The goal for this project was to bring levity and more expressive ways to communicate so users could be more themselves and have a more interactive Zoom experience.

While using Zoom, I found that it was difficult for gifs and emojis to be viewable to all users. Another issue was that posting gifs and emojis in the current Zoom chats not only required use of the sidebar but the gifs also remained there until the end of the chat. This could be a problem because what's appropriate in one moment might not fit the mood just a few seconds later. You don't want an Office gif of Michael Scott raising the roof to stay in the chat when the conversation transitions to talk of a financial downturn in the company. I tried to make a version that would have gifs and emojis that only show temporarily so that users could react to the moment.

The most difficult part of the project was coming up with different solutions. I had to think about what was possible within Zoom’s guidelines and then see where I could stretch the app. I checked in with my developer friends to make sure the solution I came up with was plausible and that it wouldn’t take an overhaul of the app to implement. Feedback from users encouraged me along the way, some saying that they only talk in gifs. Hopefully a feature similar to this can be added in the future to make for a more personal video chat experience.
