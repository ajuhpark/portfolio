OVERVIEW

Background

So you‚Äôve matched online.

It's on to chatting but for some people the text game, for lack of a better word, (it can suck).

These are some of the thoughts that can run through text sender‚Äôs heads:

  • "Should I wait to text as long as they made me wait?"
  • "I‚Äôm not interested enough to continue but I feel bad ghosting."
  • "Was my text too long or awkward? Did my text sound too short and blunt?"

Problem

Texting an online match can drag on for weeks without anything of substance being said - and that's if both parties decide not to ghost.

After matching with another user, there is room for quicker, more efficient and more intentional interactions that can reduce instances of ghosting and relieve user anxiety.

Solution

'The Ring' is a dating app that takes the game out of the game.

Once users match, they have a limited time to schedule a meeting in 'the Ring' where they can text, call or videochat for five minutes. This takes the guesswork out of texting and allows users to quickly find out about their match.

RESEARCH

User Survey

I started with research and began by creating a survey and the participants included a range of genders and gender preferences for dating. These were a couple takeaways I had.

I asked users when they feel like it was no longer appropriate to ghost someone. More than half the people marked that it was never appropriate to ghost. On the other end of the spectrum, three people marked that ghosting would be appropriate up until 2 dates - these were two men and a nonbinary person. Women had a stronger dislike for ghosting than men in the survey.

I also wanted to get a sense of how people felt about texting so I asked participants how much more thought was put into text conversations with their matches relative to their normal conversations. More than half the people responded that texts with their matches required more thought. With that in mind, I thought there was room to lessen thought and anxiety in that space and create more opportunity for natural and flowing conversations.

Competitive Analysis

For my competitive analysis, I took a look at different dating apps to see how they approached user interactions and specifically interactions after users would matched. The three I focused on were Coffee Meets Bagel, Hinge and Goat Date.

Coffee Meets Bagel

Coffee Meets Bagel encouraged users to take things slower by giving them a set amount of matches that they would have to decide on within the day. Users would have to pay more attention and think about the person‚Äôs profile instead of swiping so quickly.

I appreciated the thought behind limited interactions and slowing down the app. I wanted to bring the same intentionality to The Ring by putting time limits on user decisions, for example users would have to propose a meeting time within 48 hours.

Hinge

Hinge‚Äôs strength was its clean and modern user interface. They also offered different mediums of communication. In this screenshot, they emphasize their in-app video capabilities.

Goat Date

Goatdate is an app that focuses on video chatting after matching. After users match, they could call their match for a five minute video chat. I really liked the idea for time limited video chats and wanted to build on it by giving users a chance to schedule their meetings.

In Goatdate, users could call at any time but there‚Äôs a chance that the call comes in at a time that doesn‚Äôt work for the other user. I thought there was an opportunity there to be more intentional by setting aside a set time much like how people approach business meetings.

TESTING

After mocking up designs for the screens, I did some usability testing and I wanted to highlight a few things that I learned in that process. Here are a few examples of changes I made.

Calendar

The calendar screen was an important part of the scheduling process. In the first version, after a user would match, they would be taken to this calendar screen where they could propose a time to meet. I had instructions on the top to place two five minute time slots on Martha's calendar.

From the feedback I received, it wasn‚Äôt intuitive for the users to place the two bars on the calendar. One user wasn't sure what the yellow bars were and thought it wasn't related to the calendar because it was a different color. They also noted that there was no instruction to drag or drop the bars.

v1

So I changed the interaction instructions to the following: "Tap and place 2 time slots on Martha's calendar". I put the ‚Äò2‚Äô in bold to emphasize that they need to place two times.

In the new interaction users could tap a time on the calendar and there would be a confirm button that pops up that they could click. Once they confirm, the bar would be set there and they would be able to place another bar in the same way. This was an easier way for users to place times on the calendar.

Based on user feedback, the content in the feed needed more structure and organization. When I was user testing, a few participants were unsure of what the site was for. Because it involved different types of media, some people mistook it for a streaming service like Hulu or Netflix.

To make the purpose of the site more clear, I placed a banner at the top of the website with the site mission statement stating that it‚Äôs a website for creative fan content.

Conclusion

One of my main takeaways from this project was to make things obvious and clear from the start.

After performing user testing on my first mockup, I was surprised to see that there was confusion on the function and purpose of the app. The mixture of media and art for the content threw some people off. Because the idea of 'Take' had been brewing in my mind, I mistakenly assumed that my users would be on the same wavelength and be off and running once they clicked into it.

After seeing which parts of the app were difficult to initially grasp, I tried to make everything as obvious and clear as possible. I put a large banner with the 'Take' mission statement on the top of the website. I put the home, search and account buttons where other social media apps would normally have them. I tried to adapt features from Youtube and VSCO for the feed so the organization of the content would look familiar but the layout would still look fresh and specific to 'Take'.

'Take' was created with the foundation of user research, surveying and testing. Through different iterations, this app hopefully accomplished the goal of creating a collaborative service where users could save, organize and upload fan artwork.
